# Guide: presenting your results

This document describes the deliverables, their expected content, and the evaluation criteria for the Fanal exercise.

---

## Deliverables

Each collaboration must submit three pieces of work:

1. **Oral presentation** — 12 min + 8 min for questions about your collaboration's results.
2. **Written article** — 4–5 pages (including figures) summarising your analysis and results.
3. **Methodology report** — a short document describing how the work was carried out.

---

## 1. Oral presentation

Structure your talk following these points:

1. **Motivation and detector** — Why does this experiment exist? How does the detector work?
2. **Data and selection** — What data did you analyse? What selection cuts did you apply and why?
3. **Analysis and checks** — Describe the analysis steps. What checks did you perform to verify that your results are reasonable (e.g. does the fit reproduce the data? Are the efficiencies and event counts in a sensible range?).
4. **Results** — Present the key numerical results: estimated number of background events (Bi, Tl) with uncertainties, and the result on the signal (upper limit on the number of signal events, limit on the half-life, or evidence for a signal if found).
5. **Relevant figures** — Show and explain the plots that support your conclusions.
6. **Conclusions** — Summarise what you have measured or constrained.

**Important:** the 8-minute question period counts towards the evaluation (see *Evaluation criteria* below). Questions will come both from the instructor and from the other collaborations.

---

## 2. Inter-collaboration discussion

After all presentations, there will be a joint session where collaborations compare and discuss their results with each other.

- Each collaboration analyses a different dataset. Are your results consistent with those of the other groups?
- Can you combine information across collaborations to improve the sensitivity?
- This discussion is part of the evaluation of your understanding of the physics.

---

## 3. Written article

Follow the standard structure of a scientific publication:

1. **Author list**
2. **Abstract** — a brief summary (5–10 lines) of the analysis and the main result.
3. **Introduction** — motivation for the experiment, detector description, physics context.
4. **Analysis** — selection criteria, energy resolution, background estimation method, fit procedure, systematic checks.
5. **Results** — numerical results with uncertainties, key figures, interpretation (limit or measurement).
6. **Conclusions** — summary of findings.
7. **References**

---

## 4. Methodology report

This document should describe *how* you worked, not *what* you found. It helps us understand your process and evaluate your scientific methodology.

1. **Task distribution** — Who was responsible for what? Were tasks shared or divided? How did you coordinate?
2. **References and tools** — What materials, references, and software tools did you use?
3. **Workflow** — Describe the steps you followed: group discussions, task planning, intermediate checks, iterations.
4. **Use of AI** — If you used AI tools (e.g. ChatGPT, Claude, Copilot, ...):
   - What did you use them for? (searching references, help with code, understanding concepts, ...)
   - Include concrete examples of prompts you used.
   - Critically assess the AI's contributions: was the output correct, incomplete, or wrong? Did you have to modify it?
   - AI is a legitimate tool — we want to see that you used it *critically*, not blindly.
5. **Difficulties** — What problems did you encounter? How did you solve them?

---

## Evaluation criteria

| Component | Weight | What is evaluated |
|-----------|--------|-------------------|
| **Results** | 50% | Quality of the oral presentation and written article: correctness of numerical results, clarity of figures, soundness of conclusions. |
| **Understanding** | 30% | Depth of physics understanding, demonstrated through: answers to questions during the presentation, questions posed to other collaborations, and participation in the inter-collaboration discussion. |
| **Methodology** | 20% | Quality of the methodology report: clear task distribution, appropriate use of tools and references, critical use of AI, identification and resolution of difficulties. |
